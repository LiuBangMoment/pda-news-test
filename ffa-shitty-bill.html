The Price of Populism: the FFA's "Food Security" Bill Threatens Nigeria's Economy

The Forgotten Farmer Alliance pushes a populist agenda to tax imported food, that threatens to unravel decades of WAFTA-driven growt. Inside the fierce committee debate that exposed the "Food Security" bill as an economic hazard — and why Nigeria’s commitment to free trade will ultimately prevail.

By Hadiza Usaini · 5 June, 2058
Most of Nigeria’s agriculture is concentrated in the middle and northern parts of the country.

The foundation of Nigeria’s unprecedented prosperity over the past few decades has rested on a fundamental, undeniable truth: a stable government combined with opening up to the world guarantees growth. It is this unwavering commitment to economic openness, decisively anchored by the strategic integration provided by the West African Trade Agreement (WAFTA), that has lifted millions out of poverty and transformed our nation's economic landscape.

Yet, there are those who wish to drag us back into the dark ages of isolationism. Enter the Forgotten Farmer Alliance (FFA) and their economically illiterate flagship proposal: the Nigerian Food Security and Self-Sufficiency Bill.

A Tax on the Urban Poor

The premise of the bill is deceptively simple: it seeks to levy a staggering 15% tariff on all imported foods and foodstuffs, with a few negligible exemptions. The revenues are to be funnelled into a newly minted Agricultural Infrastructure Investment Fund (AIIF), theoretically designed to build rural roads and cold chains.

However, beneath this veneer of agrarian romanticism lies a deeply regressive agenda. The FFA, a party steeped in ethno-nationalist rhetoric that routinely scapegoats foreign workers and global integration for domestic woes, is essentially proposing a massive tax hike on ordinary citizens. By artificially stifling supply, this legislation protects the most inefficient producers at the direct expense of the consumer.

The People’s Democratic Alliance (PDA) has rightly sounded the alarm. As PDA representative John Akinboboye succinctly noted, the global market provides a cheap and affordable option to combat malnutrition. Creating additional barriers to free trade will inevitably mean that thousands of Nigerians will be priced out of basic sustenance, forcing families to sacrifice other essential expenses to cope with skyrocketing food costs. The PDA envisions a modern, high-tech Nigeria integrated into the global south; the FFA envisions a fractured, agrarian state built on xenophobia and market distortion.

Populism Meets Reality

The sheer unworkability of the bill was laid bare during a recent session of the Committee on Zonal Affairs and Intergovernmental Relations.

Francis Unongo, representing the FFA, opened the floor with a predictable, emotive manifesto. He painted a picture of the "humble farmer" supposedly under siege by "underhanded foreign trade practices," whilst conveniently ignoring that domestic agricultural inefficiencies are largely self-inflicted, as a result of a reluctance to modernise. Unongo attempted to sell the 15% tariff as a "modest response" and an "exemplar of self-restraint," a statement that borders on economic vandalism.

Fortunately, logic prevailed among the other committee members. The Southern Renewal Party’s Joseph Goldberg did not mince words, rightly labelling tariffs as "subsidies in the purest form" that transparently favour one sector while penalising consumers. Goldberg correctly identified that such protectionism merely stuffs "the rotten produce of incompetents down our throats" rather than incentivising genuine competitiveness.

"In my view, Mr. Unonga's bill, while well meaning, would have disastrous consequences for both the supply and quality of food in this country. Agricultural tariffs would reward only the laziest and most stupid farmers who are content with scrounging off other people's money. I believe a more sensible solution could be found to help diligent farmers become more productive, which would benefit both agriculture and consumers".
— Joseph Goldberg, the Southern Renewal Party

Furthermore, the geopolitical ramifications are catastrophic. Isaia Panduri of the National Democratic Front (NDF) highlighted the glaring risk of international retaliation, noting that other African Union nations would inevitably raise tariffs against Nigerian exports, crippling our hard-won international market share.

Perhaps the most damning structural critique came from the All Biafra Progressive List (ABPL). Eri Nwachukwu expertly exposed the bill's foundational paradox: if the punitive tariffs actually succeed in reducing imports, the revenue base for the much-touted infrastructure fund will shrink to nothing. If the tariffs fail to stop imports, Nigerian citizens simply foot the bill to make up the difference. In either scenario, it amounts to a draconian tax on lower-class urban Nigerians. Even ecological concerns were raised by MOTTAINAI's Williams Tyjani, who warned that the bill's animal feed exemptions would incentivise resource-heavy livestock farming, exacerbating water scarcity and methane emissions.

As the committee session adjourned, a vote on the bill was called. While the final tally is still underway, the overwhelming cross-party opposition suggests this disastrous piece of legislation is dead on arrival. Nigeria’s future lies in reform, openness, and free trade—not in the FFA’s bitter retreat into the past.

Progress Over Vulgar Protectionism

Ultimately, the solution to the genuine struggles of rural producers does not lie in the vulgar protectionism touted by the FFA. Shielding systemic inefficiencies behind a punitive tariff wall is a recipe for economic stagnation that merely treats the symptoms rather than the underlying disease. Instead, Nigeria’s focus must remain firmly fixed on the future through comprehensive economic planning and accelerated technological progress. True food security cannot be achieved by stubbornly cutting off the global market, but rather by coordinating state-driven investments that seamlessly integrate rural areas into the broader, modernised economy.

Rather than retreating into an isolationist fantasy, the government must drive agricultural modernisation by transitioning away from outdated manual labour and embracing high-tech solutions. Progress demands bold innovation, forward-thinking state investment, and a national infrastructure plan that builds resilient, modern supply chains. Nigeria's rural future depends on structured development and technological advancement, not on levying regressive taxes against the very citizens powering the nation's twenty-first-century resurgence.
