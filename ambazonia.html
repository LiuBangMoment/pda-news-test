Defying the Silence: the People's Democratic Movement is born in New Makoko

With the international community turning a blind eye to Yaoundé's abuses, Ambazonia's newly formed People's Democratic Movement is charting a radical, pragmatic, progressive path forward for the region.

By Hadiza Usaini · 4 June, 2058
A pro-Biya march in Cameroon to support the repressive policies in the Ambazonia region.

Driven from their ancestral homelands and abandoned by the international community, the Anglophone diaspora of Cameroon has fundamentally reshaped its struggle for survival. On the 1st of June, 2058, amidst a sea of red and blue flags, the People's Democratic Movement (PDM) was officially founded in New Makoko, Nigeria. Established as an independent sister party to Nigeria's progressive People's Democratic Alliance (PDA), the PDM represents a unified, progressive front against what its leaders unequivocally describe as the ongoing cultural and physical genocide of the Ambazonian people.

The timing of the congress was no coincidence. It arrived just days after a scandalous display of international apathy at the African Union. On Sunday, May 25th, the AU General Assembly failed to reach the two-thirds majority required to send a Nigerian-backed fact-finding mission to the Western Autonomous Region. Despite Nigerian President Michael Khalil’s impassioned plea that "sovereignty is not a license for silence," the motion was blocked, largely by Francophone nations shielding the regime in Yaoundé.

"Today, Nigeria and Ambazonia stand as one – the criminal actions of the French-backed Cameroonian regime are not only harming the people of Ambazonia, but also the stability of the entire region's development".
— John Akinboboye, Spokesperson for the Central Committee of the People's Democratic Party

To understand the raw emotion present at the PDM congress, one must understand a century of marginalisation. The territory formerly known as Southern Cameroons was a British Protectorate administered alongside Nigeria until a flawed 1961 UN plebiscite saw the region absorbed into the Francophone Federal Republic of Cameroon. Decades of discriminatory laws followed, culminating in a brutal civil war between 2017 and 2032.

Though the conflict nominally ended with the 2032 Port Harcourt Agreement—which guaranteed equal treatment for Anglophones — the Cameroonian government almost immediately discarded its promises. By late 2032, a military government had suspended the voting rights of English-speaking Ambazonians, shuttered their schools, and restricted Protestant worship. In 2035, the very name "Ambazonia" was legally erased from official documents, replaced by the sterile "Western Autonomous Region," and Cameroon was expelled from the Commonwealth of Nations for its gross mistreatment of the population and flagrant violations of international treaties. Today, merely flying the Ambazonian flag is a criminal offence.

Testimonies from the Congress

The atmosphere at the 174 Eternal Peace Avenue venue — the headquarters of the PDA — was electric. Many women and men, rejected by the country of their birth, yet united by their ideals and their shared suffering at the hands of the Francophone government in Yaoundé. Delegates shared harrowing accounts of the Cameroonian state's brutal attempts to erase their identity.

  • Dr. Elias Fon, a progressive intellectual and former literature professor at the University of Buea, brought the hall to tears. He recounted how state gendarmerie raided his underground English-language literature seminars. "They did not just burn our books; they arrested my students," Fon stated. "Two of my brightest pupils, barely nineteen, were taken to the Kondengui maximum security prison simply for reading in their mother tongue. We have not heard from them in four years."

  • Martha Tanyi, an Ambazonian trade unionist currently working closely with the Nigeria Federation of Trade Unions (NFTU), detailed the economic strangulation of the region. "When the agricultural workers in the Southwest tried to strike for basic safety standards in 2056, the Biya regime didn't send negotiators; they sent paramilitaries," she told the assembly. "Our union halls were firebombed. We are treated as slave labour on our own soil, enriching a Francophone elite that despises our existence."

  • Comrade Julius Ayuk, a diaspora activist advocating for a deeper integration with China, highlighted Yaoundé's weaponisation of poverty. "Any infrastructure project that might uplift the Anglophone population is systematically dismantled," Ayuk noted, describing how Cameroonian forces deliberately sabotaged a community-funded solar grid. "They want us in the dark, both literally and politically."

Almost every delegate bore personal scars from Yaoundé's criminal policies. In recent years, the Biya regime has cynically weaponised the rise of the Ambazonian Justice Army (AJA) to justify a draconian escalation in state terror. For the Cameroonian government, there is no difference between armed insurgency and peaceful civil resistance; teachers, unionists, and activists are routinely branded as "recruiters" or "civilian disruptors" working for the AJA. This calculated smear campaign grants security forces a blank cheque to crush all democratic dissent with absolute, unchecked military brutality.

Nigeria Steps Up

The Cameroonian regime's hold on the region is increasingly fragile, maintained only through the neo-colonial backing of France. Recently leaked French diplomatic cables, published on June 3rd by Le Figaro, reveal that Paris views the Biya government as "paralyzed by internal dysfunction" and entirely incapable of coordinating a coherent response without heavy French intelligence and air defence support.

Recognising this vulnerability, the PDM has adopted a robust platform. The movement demands a return to the 2032 Port Harcourt Agreement, the official recognition of the name "Ambazonia," regional self-determination, and the establishment of a consultative people's democracy. Furthermore, the PDM advocates for a "socialist market economy" and deeper economic cooperation with China and Nigeria.

The formation of the PDM has been met with open arms by its host nation. The People's Democratic Alliance formally welcomed the PDM's creation with declarations of unbreakable solidarity, allowing the movement consultative status within the PDA's own National Party Congress.

More crucially, the political winds in Lagos are shifting rapidly in the diaspora's favour. In direct response to the AU's inaction and the PDM's lobbying, the Nigerian National Assembly is currently expediting two landmark pieces of legislation:

  • The Ambazonia Liberty Resolution: A formal declaration of the Nigerian state's solidarity with the persecuted Anglophone minority, officially condemning Yaoundé's actions as genocidal.

  • The Ambazonia National Liberation Committee Bill: A sweeping legislative package designed to legally and financially coordinate opposition to the Cameroonian regime from within Nigerian borders. If passed, the bill will provide state funding for the creation of Ambazonian cultural and political institutions in exile, as well as the establishment of radio and television stations broadcasting directly from Nigeria into the Western Autonomous Region, piercing the Cameroonian state's media blackout.

For decades, the people of Ambazonia have suffered in the dark. With the birth of the People's Democratic Movement, and with the growing institutional might of the Nigerian Republic standing behind them, they are stepping into the light.
